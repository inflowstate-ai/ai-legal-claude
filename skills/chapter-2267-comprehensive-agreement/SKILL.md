# Chapter 2267 Comprehensive Agreement Reference

Chapter 2267 of the Texas Government Code is the enabling authority for public-private partnerships entered into by Texas governmental bodies to design, construct, finance, operate, or maintain "qualifying projects." The statute defines what a P3 is, who can enter one, how procurement happens, and what the resulting agreements must contain. This skill captures the statutory framework so any specific Chapter 2267 transaction can be analyzed against it.

This skill is the **statutory umbrella**. The contractual implementation (typically a ground lease and improvement lease) and the financing overlay (typically CTL) are addressed in separate skills.

## What Chapter 2267 actually authorizes

Chapter 2267 lets a "responsible governmental entity" (RGE) — defined to include municipalities, counties, school districts, special districts, state agencies, and certain authorities — enter into a partnership with a private "contracting person" to deliver a qualifying project on terms not available under conventional public procurement. The statute is permissive: it does not require an RGE to use P3 delivery, but it provides authorization that overrides ordinary procurement constraints when the RGE chooses to.

The statute's three core moves:

1. **It authorizes a single, integrated agreement** (the Comprehensive Agreement) that can include design, construction, financing, operation, and maintenance — collapsing multiple conventional procurements into one.
2. **It authorizes private financing** of public facilities, which is the structural feature that distinguishes a real Chapter 2267 P3 from a publicly financed design-build.
3. **It conditions all of this on procedural protections** — adopted P3 Guidelines, public-interest findings, defined approval pathway, mandatory CA terms — that ensure the public side has a structured process rather than ad hoc deal-making.

## The procurement pathway

A Chapter 2267 transaction follows a defined sequence. Skipping or compressing any step exposes the deal to challenge.

### 1. P3 Guidelines (§2267.052)

The RGE must adopt P3 Guidelines by Resolution before entering into a Comprehensive Agreement. The Guidelines establish the procedures for solicitation, evaluation, negotiation, and approval of P3 proposals. Confirm the Guidelines exist, were adopted in compliance with the Open Meetings Act, and reference Chapter 2267 explicitly. **Without adopted Guidelines, the RGE has no authority to enter a CA**, and any CA is vulnerable to challenge.

### 2. Solicitation — RFQ, RFP, or Unsolicited Proposal

Three pathways under the Guidelines:

- **RFQ / RFP.** RGE solicits proposals for a defined qualifying project. Standard sequence: RFQ for qualifications, shortlist, RFP for detailed proposals, evaluation, selection.
- **Unsolicited proposal.** A private party submits a proposal for a project the RGE has not solicited. The Guidelines must specify how the RGE evaluates unsolicited proposals and whether it triggers a competitive process (often required to test the market).
- **Sole-source / direct negotiation.** Permitted only on findings supporting the absence of competitive alternatives — rare and high-risk.

### 3. Interim Agreement (§2267.054)

Often used to fund and govern the predevelopment phase between selection and Comprehensive Agreement execution. Not strictly required by statute but operationally essential for any deal involving design development, environmental, financial modeling, or stakeholder engagement before binding commitment.

Key Interim Agreement issues to confirm:

- **Scope of predevelopment services and deliverables** — clearly bounded; not a back-door commitment to construction.
- **Fee structure** — typically reimbursement-based with caps; sometimes a success fee on CA execution.
- **Exclusivity** — does the developer have protection from the RGE shopping the deal during predevelopment? Confirm the IA includes a defined exclusivity period.
- **Termination rights and compensation** — what happens if the deal does not advance to CA? Compensation for sunk predevelopment cost is the developer's principal protection.
- **IP and work product ownership** — designs, models, reports developed during predevelopment. Default rule should favor the developer if the deal does not advance, with a license to the RGE.
- **City obligations and approvals required during predevelopment** — site access, information delivery, internal approvals.
- **Pathway to Comprehensive Agreement** — conditions precedent, target dates, no-binding-CA language.
- **Representations that could bind the SPE prematurely** — flag any "best efforts" or "shall enter into a CA" language; predevelopment is conditional, not committed.

### 4. Scoping Phase (operational, not statutory)

Many deals add a Scoping Phase between IA and CA, often executed under the City Manager's signature authority (commonly capped at $50K–$100K depending on the RGE's procurement charter) so it does not require separate Council action. Used to fund architect engagement, conceptual design, refined budgeting, and the final CA workup. Document the scope precisely — anything outside the City Manager's authority needs Council approval and breaks the phase structure.

### 5. Comprehensive Agreement (§2267.058)

The final binding contract. Council approval required. Must include the §2267.058 mandatory terms (next section).

## §2267.058 — mandatory terms in the Comprehensive Agreement

Section 2267.058 enumerates terms that must appear in the CA. Confirm each is addressed; omissions are statutory non-conformance and grounds for challenge.

The required CA terms cover, at a minimum:

- **Delivery date and performance schedule.** Construction and operations milestones.
- **Quality assurance, inspection, and acceptance.** Standards, testing, RGE acceptance procedures.
- **Cost, payment, and financing terms.** Including how the project is financed, what the RGE pays (lease, fee, user charges), and any reimbursement.
- **User fees, lease payments, or other revenue arrangement.** Calculation, escalation, payment mechanics.
- **Reimbursement to the RGE.** For predevelopment cost, oversight, public-interest expenses where applicable.
- **Default and remedies.** Cure rights, termination triggers, damages.
- **Liability allocation and indemnification.** Including limits and insurance.
- **Public-interest protections.** Required by statute; see next section.
- **Term and termination.** Including reversion and exit.
- **Material modification procedures.** How amendments are made and approved.

If any of these is missing or addressed only by reference to a separate document, flag it. The CA is the statutory deliverable; the §2267.058 terms must be in it or properly incorporated.

## Public-interest protections

Chapter 2267 requires the CA to address "public interest" — a deliberately broad concept. In practice, public-interest protections include:

- **Reversion at termination.** Improvements transfer to the RGE at lease/agreement end, free and clear. **This is the public ownership pathway under a properly structured P3 — not a purchase option** (a purchase option has independent constitutional, tax, and lender problems addressed in the CTL skill).
- **Performance security.** Construction performance and payment bonds; sometimes O&M performance security.
- **Step-in and cure rights.** RGE's right to assume operations or contract performance on developer default.
- **Service-level standards.** Where the deal includes operations.
- **Public access and use protections.** Where applicable.
- **Compliance with public-purpose covenants.** Use restrictions consistent with the project's stated purpose.

Confirm each protection is drafted with a real enforcement mechanism. "Reversion at termination" without a quitclaim or transfer mechanic is just a sentence.

## Constitutional debt overlay

A Chapter 2267 P3 must be structured so that the RGE's payment obligations do not constitute "debt" requiring voter approval under Texas Constitution Article III §49 (state) and Article XI §5 (municipalities). This is the structural constraint that drives the most consequential lease drafting decisions.

Two compatible mechanisms:

- **Annual appropriation.** Payments contingent on annual legislative appropriation. Simple; common in conventional municipal lease-purchase. **Generally fatal to CTL execution** because the lender cannot underwrite a payment stream subject to a budget-cycle walk-away. See the CTL skill for full analysis.
- **Use-based abatement.** Payments contingent on continued beneficial use and occupancy of the facility. Abatement triggered only by actual vacation and surrender — not by a budget vote. **The CTL-compatible structure** and the developer-favorable position. The obligation is current-year and use-dependent, satisfying the constitutional "not debt" test, while the payment stream is reliable enough to underwrite.

Read every Chapter 2267 CA against this distinction. Use-based abatement is the answer; annual appropriation language anywhere in the CA, the leases, or supporting documents must be challenged and reframed.

## Texas Public Information Act exposure

Information in the RGE's custody is presumptively public under Chapter 552 of the Government Code. Several provisions matter for Chapter 2267 deals:

- **§552.153 — P3-specific exception.** Excepts proprietary records and trade secrets provided by a proposer to the RGE under Chapter 2267, including financial records and competitive bid work product. Subject to a **§552.153(c) carve-out** that makes "the terms of any interim or comprehensive agreement" disclosable, including financing arrangements involving public money.
- **§552.110 — trade secrets and commercial information.** General exception. Requires affirmative assertion and specific factual evidence of trade-secret status or substantial competitive harm.
- **§552.0222 — contracting information.** Treats certain contracting information as public regardless of trade-secret claims, including "any term describing the overall or total price the governmental body will or could potentially pay." Total lease payments under a CA are disclosable; internal cost decomposition is not.
- **§552.104 — competitive bidding exception.** Available to private parties post-*Boeing v. Paxton*; useful supplemental protection.
- **§552.305 — third-party notice.** Requires the RGE to notify affected proposers when a TPIA request implicates their proprietary interests, allowing them to assert exceptions before the Attorney General.

The strategic principle for the developer: **don't put proprietary information in the RGE's custody.** Mark anything that does cross over with the relevant statutory citations. Build TPIA notification and cooperation provisions into the IA and CA. (See the CTL skill for the related analysis on developer loan documents and "developer fee" decomposition.)

## Prevailing wage analysis (Texas Gov't Code §2258)

§2258 requires payment of locally prevailing wages on public-works projects. The trigger is the funding source.

| Funding source | §2258 application |
|---|---|
| Developer's private capital (taxable CTL) | **Does not apply** — private money, not public funds |
| Tax-exempt conduit / nonprofit issuer bonds | **Likely applies** — tax-exempt bond proceeds are arguably public funds; Attorney General opinions and case law support application |
| RGE direct financing or governmental funds | **Applies unambiguously** under §2258.021 |

A push from the public side toward conduit financing therefore creates a hidden cost: subcontractor pricing increases under §2258 may offset the apparent rate advantage of tax-exempt execution. Flag this in any conduit-financing analysis.

## Council / governing body approval mechanics

The CA must be approved by the RGE's governing body in compliance with:

- **Open Meetings Act (Chapter 551).** Posting, notice, deliberation in open session except where statutory executive-session exceptions apply (§551.071 attorney-client, §551.072 real property, §551.087 economic development negotiations among the most relevant).
- **Local procurement rules.** RGE's charter and ordinances may impose additional notice, public hearing, or supermajority requirements.
- **Recordkeeping.** Vote recorded, agenda items properly described.

A common failure mode: substantive deal terms negotiated in executive session under §551.072 (real property) or §551.087 (economic development) are not memorialized publicly until CA execution, creating both Open Meetings exposure and TPIA exposure on the underlying records. Both should be addressed in the procedural plan, not after the fact.

## Default, cure, and exit

The CA's default and exit mechanics interact with both the leases and the CTL loan documents. Read them as a coordinated set, not in isolation.

- **Developer default.** RGE remedies typically include cure-period notice, step-in, contract assumption, and ultimately termination. Lender step-in and cure rights must be coordinated — the lender wants notice and a cure period at least as long as the RGE's, plus the right to substitute a replacement developer.
- **RGE default.** Limited in practice — the public party rarely "defaults" in a meaningful operational sense, but failure to perform CA obligations (site access, approvals, payment) must have defined consequences. Damages tend to be the only realistic remedy.
- **Termination for convenience.** Generally adverse to the developer if unilateral; if included, require full cost recovery including unrecovered predevelopment investment, debt prepayment make-whole, and equity return.
- **Reversion.** At termination (whatever the cause), improvements revert to the RGE per the agreement and the ground lease. Reversion mechanics (deed, quitclaim, certificate) must be operative, not aspirational.

## Deal-killers and structural risks

Recurring structural problems in Chapter 2267 transactions:

| Problem | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| P3 Guidelines not adopted before CA | §2267.052 violation; CA vulnerable to challenge |
| §2267.058 mandatory terms missing or addressed only by reference | Statutory non-conformance |
| Annual appropriation walk-away in payment terms | Defeats CTL execution; collapses private financing |
| Purchase option in favor of RGE | Constitutional debt risk + installment-sale tax recharacterization + CTL prohibition (see CTL skill) |
| Conduit / nonprofit issuer financing substitution | Strips developer of equitable ownership and finance return; converts P3 to conventional procurement (see CTL skill) |
| Public-interest protections without enforcement mechanism | Statutory non-conformance in substance |
| Open Meetings Act / executive session deficiencies | Procedural challenge to CA approval |
| TPIA exposure not contractually addressed | Proprietary information at risk; developer cannot rely on verbal assurances |
| §2258 prevailing wage analysis missing | Hidden cost in tax-exempt or conduit structures |
| Reversion drafted without operative transfer mechanism | "Public ownership pathway" is illusory |

## Review framework

When applying this skill to a Chapter 2267 document:

1. **Identify the document.** Interim Agreement, Scoping Agreement, Comprehensive Agreement, or amendment? Apply the right framework.
2. **Confirm the statutory authority chain.** P3 Guidelines adopted, RGE authorized, qualifying project properly characterized, procurement pathway followed.
3. **Walk §2267.058.** Each mandatory term — addressed or not. Flag omissions.
4. **Test the constitutional debt structure.** Annual appropriation or use-based abatement? If appropriation, the deal is structurally at risk.
5. **Test public-interest protections.** Reversion mechanism operative? Performance security adequate? Step-in rights real?
6. **Test TPIA exposure.** Confidentiality provisions, notification provisions, statutory exception citations in supporting documents.
7. **Test prevailing wage trigger.** Funding source mapped to §2258 applicability.
8. **Test procedural compliance.** Open Meetings Act, executive session, notice, vote.
9. **Coordinate with implementation and financing.** Lease structure (ground/improvement) and CTL terms must be consistent with the CA. Cross-reference the relevant skills.
10. **Rank issues.** Statutory non-conformance > structural risk > drafting cleanup. Don't list issues without ranking them.

## What this skill does NOT cover

- **The two-lease implementation** (ground lease + improvement lease drafting, term mechanics, equitable ownership splits, leasehold mortgage protections) — separate skill.
- **CTL loan terms and lender mechanics** — separate skill.
- **Federal tax structuring** (depreciation, true-lease analysis, §179D and similar incentives) — separate analysis.
- **Equity / SPE governance** (operating agreement, partnership terms, member protections) — separate analysis.
- **Detailed Texas constitutional debt jurisprudence beyond the appropriation/use-based-abatement framework** — this is local-counsel work and rapidly evolving with each AG opinion and appellate decision.
- **Other Texas P3-adjacent statutes** — Local Government Corporations under Transportation Code Chapter 431 Subchapter D, nonprofit housing finance corporations under Local Government Code Chapter 394, Tax Increment Reinvestment Zones under Tax Code Chapter 311, and Local Government Code §212.171 development agreements (subdivision/vested-rights) are different products with different rules. Do not treat any of these as Chapter 2267 substitutes.

When any of these intersects the Chapter 2267 work, name the gap explicitly and pull the relevant skill or counsel in.
